Apparently the distribution of the sample responds to an approximate Normal distribution. The key parameters are the sample mean of 68.36 and sample standard deviation of 11.16.

The data we have at hand is the one coming from the sample. It's a shame we don't have it from the population, but there's still hope! We can draw approximated calculations from the sample following the hypothesis testing method:

  1. Set hypothesis:
    H(null) : = 75
    H(1) : > 75

  2. Alpha:
    We'll set an alfa of 0.05 as significance threshold.

  3. Sample statistics: -4.2

4. Critical value:
Since the sample size is greater than 30, we use the Z-table to find it: -1.645

  1. We can see that the Z value -4.2 lays below the critical point of -1.645. Then we can conclude that the null hypothesis can be rejected meaning that most likely the population mean is not beyond the 75 objective we have set.

P-value approach:¶

If we had a laptop available, we could've used it to calculate the P-value and follow the p-value approach to reach to the same conclusion:

In [87]:
st.ttest_1samp(paces, 75)
Out[87]:
Ttest_1sampResult(statistic=-4.206198773846341, pvalue=0.00011006707248470678)

After calculating the P-value, we see its value is 0.01%, way under the alpha of 5%. This confirms that the we can reject the null hypothesis. In other words, the actual population mean must be much lower than the pace target of 70.

We can get rid of any doubt by actually calculating the mean of the whole population:

In [88]:

Out[88]:

Effectively, the mean of the population was much lower than the 75 we are aiming for. The mean of the sample before (68.36), did reflect well the population mean.

For us, this means that our Spanish league's average pace is still to low and we'll prompt the coaches of all teams to make an extra effort and improve the physical trainings in order to better this stat.

In order to increase the pace of the players of the Spanish League, the organizers have the gut feeling that it must be due to average excessive weight. To do so, they'll take the same sample of 50 players, and try to figure out if there's a linear relationship between "pace" and "weights", and try to conclude if the result can be extrapolated to the whole population:

In [120]:
weights = sample.weight_kg
sns.set_style("whitegrid")
ax1 = sns.scatterplot(x=weights, y=paces)    #grafic

With the naked eye, based on the sample it seems that there could slightly be a correlation, where the players with more weight run slower than the lighter. Let's double check it with the test "Pearsons Correlation Coefficient":

Hipotesis:

  • H(null) : "pace" and "weights" = independent
  • H(1) : "pace" and "weights" independent
  • Significance: 0.05
In [90]:
from scipy.stats import pearsonr
stat, p = pearsonr(paces, weights)
print('stat=%.3f, p=%.3f' % (stat, p))
if p > 0.05:
	print('Probably independent')
else:
	print('Probably dependent')

stat=-0.404, p=0.004
Probably dependent

After carrying out the test, the p-value resulted in 0.4%, way lower than the 5% minimum to back up the null hypothesis. This means we can reject the null hypothesis, so that the attributes "pace" and "weights" must be correlated.

- Exercici 3¶

Continua amb el conjunt de dades de tema esportiu que t'agradi i selecciona tres atributs del conjunt de dades. Calcula el p-valor i digues si rebutja la hiptesi nulla agafant un alfa de 5%.

So now it seems we have a clue about the general cause of the mean pace of our league being so slow: some players have overweight! We need to inform the coaches and start working hard to lose weight.

But before we start, we want to make sure this is an issue spread across all the teams in the league. So we assume that all the teams share the same mean weight. To prove it, we'll follow the cluster sampling method and take as samples three teams very different size-wise: FC Barcelona, Real Sociedad and Elche CF. Then we'll run an Analysis of variance test (ANOVA) that will calculate the p-value of all three having roughly the same mean:

Hipotesis:

  • H(null) : the means of the samples are equal
  • H(1) : one or more of the means of the samples are unequal
  • Significance: 0.05
In [122]:
 #filtering only the 3 clubs
clubs = fifa[fifa.club_name.isin(["FC Barcelona","Real Sociedad","Elche CF"])]

elx_w = clubs[clubs.club_name=="Elche CF"].weight_kg

#launching the test
from scipy.stats import f_oneway
stat, p = f_oneway(fcb_w, rsc_w, elx_w)

if p > 0.05:

else:
	print('Probably different distributions')

Probably the same distribution
